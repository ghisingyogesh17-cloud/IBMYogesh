Gangalal Shrestha

Gangalal Shrestha (1919 – 28 January 1941) was a Nepalese revolutionary who was executed by the autocratic Rana regime. He is deeply honored as one of the four foundational martyrs of the Nepalese revolution. He was an active member of the clandestine political organization named Nepal Praja Parishad, which initiated the historic movement in 1939 to dissolve the Rana oligarchy and establish a democratic system in Nepal.

Early Life

Ganga Lal was born to a well-to-do family, the son of Bhakta Lal and Tulsi Maya Shrestha. His father was a government official stationed in Ramechhap, where he served as the chief of the local Revenue Office. Upon returning to Kathmandu, Bhakta Lal renounced his share of the ancestral property in Chhetrapati and purchased a house in Pyaphal, directly adjacent to Kathmandu Durbar Square. Ganga Lal lived in this house with his family and his wife, Hasana Devi (1918–2011).

During his youth, Ganga Lal traveled to Kolkata, India, with his uncle to pursue his studies. Upon returning to Kathmandu, he enrolled in Durbar High School. He was recognized as an exceptionally brilliant student, securing a first-division ranking in his Matriculation examinations. Following this success, he admitted to Tri-Chandra College to study science.

Political Career

Ganga Lal possessed a remarkable gift for public speaking and was an eloquent orator. He actively participated in organizations dedicated to ousting the Rana dynasty and establishing democratic governance in Nepal. He was an integral member of the Mahabir School, an institution focused on fostering strong patriotic feelings and civic duty among young students.

His advocacy led to a brief arrest in 1939 for giving an impassioned public lecture in Asan, Kathmandu. Following his meeting with fellow revolutionary Dashrath Chand, he became deeply involved in the secret operations and initiatives of the Nepal Praja Parishad.

Legacy

Today, Ganga Lal is revered as one of the four key martyrs of the democratic movement that ultimately dismantled the ruling Rana regime. Every year, Nepal celebrates Martyrs' Day on January 30 to pay solemn tribute to their ultimate sacrifice.

In 1965, Nepal's Postal Services Department issued a commemorative postage stamp featuring the portraits of all four martyrs. In honor of his contribution, Ganga Path, a primary road in central Kathmandu, bears his name. Additionally, the Shahid Gangalal National Heart Center, a premier specialist cardiovascular hospital in Kathmandu, is dedicated to his memory, and his statue is prominently installed on the Shahid Gate (Martyrs' Gate) monument in Kathmandu.
